My Favorite Games

In my free time, I enjoy playing various games. Since it’s far too difficult to choose just one as my all-time favorite, I’ll be discussing both my favorite board game and my favorite video game.

Favorite Board Game: Pandemic

(Image Credit: BoardGameGeek & Z-Man Games)

Before college, my knowledge of board games was limited to the classics: Monopoly, The Game of Life, Clue, Scrabble, etc. When I joined The Grid, Baruch’s one-and-only tabletop gaming club, I was surprised to discover there was much more to the genre. In the club, I was introduced to a variety of board games, including King of Tokyo, Sheriff of Nottingham, Settlers of Catan, Exploding Kittens, and Unstable Unicorns. My current favorite board game is Pandemic (yes, I am aware of the irony), which The Grid doesn’t own. The club does own Pandemic: Reign of Cthulhu, a Lovecraftian-inspired version, which is how I was first exposed to the franchise. My friends and I played the original Pandemic for the first time at a board game café called Sip N’ Play, and even though we lost, we had a lot of fun! I liked the game so much I even decided to buy Pandemic as a birthday present for myself this year.

If you’re unfamiliar with Pandemic, it’s a strategic game for 2-4 players where the goal is to cure 4 diseases before time runs out. What I like about this game is that it is cooperative, meaning everyone must work together to win. In most board games I’ve played, players are always competing against each other. Even if two or more people join forces against someone else, like in King of Tokyo or Unstable Unicorns, those alliances are temporary since only one player can ultimately win the game. However, in Pandemic, because everyone either wins or loses as a team, there is no incentive to stab your friends in the back. Rather than competing against each other, you’re competing against the board and the clock! The game is also unforgiving, as infections can quickly spiral out of control if you don’t manage them properly. Since they occur after every player’s turn, every decision matters. What I also like about Pandemic is even though it’s more complex than most casual games, it’s also easy to learn because every turn consists of the same three phases, and turns repeat around the table until the players either win or lose.

If you’re looking for a board game with strategic depth but is also easy to learn, I highly recommend Pandemic! Maybe wait until after the pandemic to buy a copy for yourself, though…

Favorite Video Game: Pokémon SoulSilver

(Image Credit: Nintendo, Game Freak, & The Pokémon Company)

The first video games I ever played were Super Mario Land and Pokémon Silver on my sister’s Game Boy Color (spoiler: I never finished either). Since then, I’ve played a variety of video games across the Game Boy Advance SP, the Nintendo DS and DSi, the 3DS, and the Nintendo Switch. If I had to pick just one video game from my collection as my favorite, I would choose Pokémon SoulSilver, for the Nintendo DS.

If you’ve never heard of Pokémon, it's a massive media franchise centered around cute, collectible creatures called—you guessed it—Pokémon. Each entry in the main series takes place in a different region, but the goal is always the same: choose one of three starters (Fire, Water, or Grass) and set out on a journey to become the strongest Trainer in the region by catching other Pokémon, training them to be strong in battle, and defeating Gym Leaders and other Trainers.

One reason Pokémon SoulSilver is special is because it was the first Pokémon game I ever finished. Another reason it's my favorite video game is because of all the content Game Freak (the company who designs the main Pokémon games) managed to cram into it. Pokémon Gold and Pokémon Silver are unique in that they are sequels to the first set of Pokémon games, Pokémon Red and Pokémon Blue. As a result, after completing your journey in Johto, the region featured in Gold and Silver, you can continue your journey in Kanto, the region from Red and Blue, which culminates in a mountaintop 6-on-6 battle against the player character from Red and Blue! Not only is this impressive from a game design perspective, but it’s also impressive from a hardware perspective. Despite Gold and Silver being released in 1999, Game Freak was able to fit a game almost twice as large as its previous titles onto a single cartridge. Pokémon SoulSilver, which is a Nintendo DS remake of Pokémon Silver, retains the same story campaign while also greatly improving the graphics and user interface. It’s disappointing that most of the mainline Pokémon titles since then have lacked that same ambition (I’ve heard Pokémon Black 2 and White 2 breaks that trend), even as they made the jump to home consoles with Pokémon Sword and Pokémon Shield. Regardless, Pokémon SoulSilver remains one of my favorite video games of all time.
